Executive Summary

The sample template contains repeatable risks that can delay cash collection, expand unpaid support, weaken renewals, and create credit or security-review blockers. Highest-priority changes: move payment away from subjective acceptance, define support/change-order boundaries, add renewal protection, cap credits, and prepare a reusable data/security response.

  • Business owner: sales, delivery, finance, and operations coordination.
  • Legal boundary: counsel approves final contract language.
  • Recommended route: apply fallback positions before markup, then track counsel-approved language for reuse.

Priority Findings

Payment delay - severity 5/5
Customer will pay invoices Net 90 after acceptance by the project owner. Acceptance may be delayed until all internal stakeholders approve the deliverables.

Why it matters: cash collection depends on buyer-controlled acceptance and long payment terms.

Fallback: Net 15/30 from invoice date, milestone billing, or deemed acceptance after a fixed review window.

Scope creep - severity 4/5
Provider will support connected systems, data cleanup, and enablement required for Customer's go-to-market operations.

Why it matters: broad included-support language can expand work without expanding fees.

Fallback: define included support hours, excluded systems, customer dependencies, and change-order triggers.

Renewal loss - severity 4/5
The subscription renews month-to-month after launch and Customer may terminate for convenience with 30 days notice.

Why it matters: recurring revenue can disappear before delivery costs are recovered.

Fallback: annual renewal, longer notice, transition fees, or a success review before termination.

Credit exposure - severity 3/5
If the service is unavailable for more than four hours in a calendar month, Customer may request service credits against future invoices.

Why it matters: broad credit language can erase earned revenue or create uncapped concessions.

Fallback: measurable SLA misses, notice/cure window, and credits capped to fees paid for affected service.

Security blocker - severity 3/5
Customer may require Provider to delete all data within five business days and complete additional security reviews before launch.

Why it matters: security or data terms can delay signature if the response is not prepared.

Fallback: standard security appendix, DPA, subprocessors, incident process, and realistic deletion timeline.

Reusable IP - severity 3/5
All deliverables, configurations, reports, templates, and materials become Customer property upon creation.

Why it matters: reusable methods and templates can be assigned away unintentionally.

Fallback: customer owns final bespoke deliverables; provider retains pre-existing IP, tools, know-how, and reusable methods.

Buyer-Safe Summary

  • Route payment/acceptance, support scope, renewal, credit, security, and IP clauses through business review before legal markup.
  • Use fallback positions as business instructions for counsel, not as final legal language.
  • Track accepted fallback language in a reusable clause playbook for future SOWs and order forms.
  • Keep confidential contracts out of public GitHub issues; use redacted excerpts or private intake after approval.
